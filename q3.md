# Q3 — Computing the reconstruction error of Truncated SVD

Once you cut the SVD down to rank $k$, you naturally want to know how much you actually lost by doing that. That's what the reconstruction error measures — the gap between the original matrix and the approximation you built from it.

## Setup

Let $A_{d\times n}$ be the original matrix with full SVD

$$
A = U \Sigma V^T, \qquad \Sigma = \text{diag}(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \dots, \sigma_r), \quad \sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \dots \ge \sigma_r \ge 0
$$

where $r$ is the rank of $A$. The rank-$k$ truncated approximation is

$$
A_k = U_k \Sigma_k V_k^T
$$

built from only the top $k$ singular values and their corresponding singular vectors.

## The error itself

The natural way to measure "how different are these two matrices" is the Frobenius norm of their difference:

$$
\|A - A_k\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i,j} \left(A_{ij} - (A_k)_{ij}\right)^2}
$$

The elegant part is that you don't actually need to subtract the two matrices entry by entry to get this number. Because of how SVD is structured, it turns out that

$$
\|A - A_k\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i=k+1}^{r} \sigma_i^2}
$$

In words: the reconstruction error is exactly the norm of the singular values you threw away. This follows from the Eckart–Young theorem, which also tells you something reassuring — $A_k$ isn't just *a* rank-$k$ approximation, it's provably the *best possible* rank-$k$ approximation of $A$ in this norm. No other rank-$k$ matrix can get closer to $A$ than the truncated SVD does.

This is also exactly why the elbow-point idea from Q2 works: the singular values you cut off after the elbow are small, so $\sum_{i=k+1}^{r}\sigma_i^2$ stays small, which means the reconstruction error stays small even though you've thrown away most of the dimensions.

## Relative error

In practice it's often more useful to report a *relative* reconstruction error rather than the raw Frobenius norm, since the raw number depends on the scale of the data:

$$
\text{relative error} = \frac{\|A - A_k\|_F}{\|A\|_F} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=k+1}^{r}\sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{r}\sigma_i^2}}
$$

This gives a number between 0 and 1 (0% to 100%) that you can use directly to pick $k$: for example, "keep enough singular values so that the relative reconstruction error stays under 5%."
