# Q4 — Euclidean Distance vs. Cosine Similarity

Once every document or word has been turned into a numeric vector (whether that's a raw bag-of-words vector, a TF-IDF vector, or an embedding from the latent space produced by LSI), you need some way to say how "similar" two vectors are. Euclidean distance and cosine similarity are the two most common answers to that question, and they capture genuinely different notions of similarity.

## Euclidean Distance

For two vectors $x, y \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$
d(x, y) = \|x - y\|_2 = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - y_i)^2}
$$

This is just the ordinary straight-line distance between the two points, the same formula you'd use in 2D or 3D geometry, generalized to $n$ dimensions.

- **Minimum:** $d(x,y) = 0$, achieved exactly when $x = y$ — the two vectors are identical.
- **Maximum:** unbounded ($+\infty$). There's no ceiling; the farther apart the points sit in space, the larger the distance grows without limit.

So a distance close to 0 means "these are basically the same vector," and larger distances mean progressively less similar, with no fixed scale to compare against — you always need to interpret a distance relative to other distances in the same dataset.

## Cosine Similarity

For the same two vectors:

$$
\cos(\theta) = \frac{x \cdot y}{\|x\| \, \|y\|} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i y_i}{\sqrt{\sum_i x_i^2}\sqrt{\sum_i y_i^2}}
$$

This is the cosine of the angle $\theta$ between the two vectors. Instead of asking "how far apart are these points," it asks "how aligned is their direction," and it deliberately ignores the length (magnitude) of the vectors.

- **Maximum:** $\cos(\theta) = 1$, when $\theta = 0°$ — the two vectors point in exactly the same direction (one is a positive scalar multiple of the other).
- **Minimum:** $\cos(\theta) = -1$, when $\theta = 180°$ — the vectors point in exactly opposite directions. In practice, for text data where entries are non-negative counts or TF-IDF weights, the value can never go below 0, so the effective range becomes $[0, 1]$, with 0 meaning the vectors are orthogonal — they share no weighted terms at all.

## Why the difference matters

The key distinction is that cosine similarity is invariant to scale, while Euclidean distance is not. Imagine two documents about the exact same topic, using the same words in the same proportions, but one is a short paragraph and the other is a long article that repeats those same words many more times. Their word vectors point in essentially the same direction, so cosine similarity will correctly report them as very similar (close to 1). But because the long document's vector has much larger magnitude, the raw Euclidean distance between the two vectors can be quite large, incorrectly suggesting they're dissimilar.

That's why cosine similarity is the standard choice for comparing text and embedding vectors, where document length varies but topical direction is what actually matters. Euclidean distance is more appropriate when the *absolute* position or magnitude of the vectors genuinely carries meaning — for instance, comparing physical measurements or coordinates, where two points with the same direction but different magnitudes really are different.
